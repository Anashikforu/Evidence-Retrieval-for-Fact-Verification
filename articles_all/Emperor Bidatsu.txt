Traditional history
Historians consider details about the life of Emperor Bidatsu to be wikt:possible|possibly legendary, but wikt:probable|probable.Kelly, Charles F. 
He was the second son of Emperor Kimmei.  His mother, Ishi-hime, was a daughter of Emperor Senka.

His palace in Yamato Province was called Osada no Miya of Iware.

Although he had many children, none of them would ever become emperor.Richard Ponsonby-Fane|Ponsonby-Fane, Richard. (1959) ''The Imperial House of Japan,'' p. 46. According to ''Gukanshō'', Bidatsu had four empresses and 16 Imperial children (6 sons and 10 daughters).

Events of Bidatsu's life
In the 15th year of Kimmei's reign, Bidatsu was named Crown Prince.

In the 32nd year of Kimmei's reign (572), the emperor died; and his successor was Bidatsu. This was wikt:confirm|confirmed in Ceremony|ceremonies.Varley, p. 44; compare Imperial Household Agency (''Kunaichō''), 
Bidatsu's reign was marked by power struggles about Buddhism.  The two most important men in the court of Bidatsu were Soga no Umako and Mononobe no Moriya. Soga supported the growth of Buddhism, and Moriya wanted to stop it.

Bidatsu suffered from an unknown disease which afflicted him with sores.  Probably, he was the first royal victim of smallpox in Japan.Hopkins, Donald R. (2002).
