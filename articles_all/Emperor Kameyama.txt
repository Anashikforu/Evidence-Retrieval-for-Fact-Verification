Traditional history

He was the seventh son of Emperor Go-Saga and the younger brother of Emperor Go-Fukakusa.Martin, Peter. (1997). ''The Chrysanthemum Throne: a History of the Emperors of Japan,'' p. 81.

Kameyama was the father of 36 children, including the son and heir who became Emperor Go-Uda.Martin, pp. 81-82.

Events of Kameyama's life
 '''1258''' (''Shōka 2''): Kameyama's son, Prince Tsunehito was named Crown Prince and heir at age nine.
 '''1259''' (''Shōgen 1, 11th month''): In the 14th year of Emperor Go-Fukakusa|Go-Fukakusa's reign, he abdication|abdicated.  Go-Fukakusa's younger brother got the succession (senso).Titsingh,  '''1268''' (''Bun'ei 5''): Kameyama did not answer a letter from Kublai Khan which demanded tribute.  Khan, the leader of China, saw this non-response as rude and not respectful.
 '''1274''' (''Bun'ei 11, 1st month''): In the 15th year of Kameyama's reign, he abdication|abdicated.Titsingh,  '''19 November 1274''' (''Bun'ei 11, 20th day of the 10th month''): Yuan Dynasty|Yuan China (Kublai Khan) sent a Fleet (ships)|fleet and an army to invade Japan. Some military forces landed near Fukuoka in Kyūshū.   This was the "Battle of Bun'ei" or the 1st Mongol Invasion.  The same day, a storm sank many of the ships with the main part of the invading army. The invaders ran away to Korea.Davis, Paul K. (2001).  '''1281''' (''Kōan (Kamakura period)|Kōan 4''): This is called the "Battle of Kōan" or the 2nd Mongol Invasion. A typhoon broke up the invading fleet; and this act of 'divine wind' was called ''kamekaze.''
 '''1291''' (''Shōō (Kamakura period)|Shōō 4''): Kameyama helped to found the Buddhism|Buddhist temple Nanzen-ji in Kyōto.Martin, p. 81


 '''1305''' (''Kagen 3''): Kameyama died.
