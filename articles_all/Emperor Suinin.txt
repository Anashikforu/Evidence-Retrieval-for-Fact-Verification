The ''Gukanshō'' records that Suinin he ruled from the palace of ''Tamaki-no-miya'' at Makimuku in what will come to be known as Yamato province.Brown, p. 253.

Traditional history
Suinin is almost certainly a wikt:legend|legend; but the ''Kojiki'' and ''Nihonshoki'' record his name and genealogy. He was the third son of Emperor Sujin.

Events of Suinin's life
The limited information about Suinin does not imply that no such person ever existed. Very little information is wikt:available|available for study prior to the reign of the 29th monarch, Emperor Kimmei.Titsingh, pp. 34-36; Brown, pp. 261-262; Varley, pp. 123-124. 

One of Suinin's daughters was Yamatohime-no-mikoto.  She established a site for  ceremonies honouring the Shinto sun goddess (Amaterasu Omikami).Ponsonby-Fane, ''Imperial House,'' p. 32.  In the region of Ise, Mie|Ise, she founded the Ise Shrine.Brown, p. 253. She became the first Saiō at Ise.

The records of Asama Shrine at the base of Mount Fuji describe events in this time frame. The earliest Shinto ceremonies in honour of Konohanasakuya-hime were in the 3rd year of the reign of Emperor Suinin (垂仁天皇3年).Ponsonby-Fane, Richard. (1962).  ''Studies in Shinto and Shrines,'' p.458.

''Nihonshoki'' describes a wrestling match during his era.  This was the origin of Sumo|Sumo wrestling.Aston, William. (1998). ''Nihongi,'' Vol. 1, pp. 167-187.
