The '''migration period''', or '''barbarian invasions''' or '''Völkerwanderung''', happened in AD 300–700 in Europe,Precise dates given may vary; ones that are often cited are 410, the sack of Rome by Alaric I, or 751, the accession of Pippin the Short and the establishment of the Carolingian dynasty.''Climate change and human mobility: challenges to the social sciences'', eds. Kirsten Hastrup, Karen Fog Olwig (Cambridge; New York: Cambridge University Press, 2012), p. 29. at the end of ancient history and during the Early Middle Ages.


The migrations included the Goths, Vandals, Franks, and other Germanic tribes|Germanic, Bulgar, and Slavic peoples|Slavic tribes.John Bagnell Bury, ''A History of the Later Roman Empire: From Arcadius to Irene (395 A.D. to 800 A.D.)'' (London; New York: Macmillan and Company, 1889), p. 31 They may have been influenced by attacks of the Huns in the East and may also be connected to the Turkic migrations in Central Asia, overpopulation, or climate changes. The migration period also included groups of Angles, Saxons, Frisians, and some Jutes to Britain.Peter Hunter Blair; Simon Keynes, ''An Introduction to Anglo-Saxon England'' (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2003), p. 10

Migrations would continue well into the Middle Ages, beyond 1000 AD, with successive waves of Slavs, Alans, Eurasian Avars|Avars, Bulgars, Hungarians, Pechenegs, Cumans, and Tatars that changed the ethnic makeup of Eastern Europe.Lucy Mallows, ''Transylvania'' (Chalfont St. Peter: Bradt Travel Guides, 2008), p. 18 Western European historians, however, tend to stress the migrations that were most relevant to Western Europe.
