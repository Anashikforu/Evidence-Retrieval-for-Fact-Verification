 Traditional history 
The ''Kojiki'' and ''Nihon Shoki'' record that Ankō was the second son of Emperor Ingyō.

Some accounts report that Ankō murdered his own father.Martin, Peter. (1997). 
 Events of Ankō's life 
Very little is known about the events of Ankō's life and reign.  Only limited information is wikt:available|available for study prior to the reign of the 29th monarch, Emperor Kimmei.Titsingh, pp. 34-36; Brown, pp. 261-262; Varley, pp. 123-124.

 '''462''': According to Chinese records, a delegation from the Japanese monarch was received in the court of the southern Sung Dynasty.  The "king" mentioned in these documents probably would have been Emperor Ankō.Nussbaum, Louis Frédéric ''et al.'' (2005). 

It is believed that Ankō was murdered in the third year of his reign.Aston, William. (1998). ''Nihongi,'' Vol. 1, pp. 328-333.

= After his death =
This emperor's official name after his death (his posthumous name) was wikt:regularize|regularized many centuries after the lifetime which was wikt:ascribe|ascribed to Ankō.Aston (1998), pp. 146-147.

According to the Imperial Household Agency, the emperor's final resting place is in an wikt:earthen|earthen tumulus (''kofun'').Edwards, Walter.
