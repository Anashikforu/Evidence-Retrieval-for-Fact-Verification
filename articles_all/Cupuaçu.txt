

'''Cupuaçu''' (''Theobroma grandiflorum''), also spelled '''Cupuassu''' and '''Copoasu''', is a tropical rainforest tree related to Cacao. Common throughout the Amazon basin, it is widely cultivated in the north of Brazil. The largest production occurs in  Pará, followed by Amazonas (Brazil)|Amazonas, Rondônia and Acre (state)|Acre.

Cupuaçu trees usually are between  5 and 15 meters (16 to 50 feet) tall. Some trees can reach 20 meters (65&nbsp;feet). They have brown :wikt:bark|bark. Their leaf|leaves are 25–35&nbsp;cm (10–14&nbsp;in) long and 6–10&nbsp;cm (2–4&nbsp;in) across. These leaves have 9 or 10 pairs of veins. As they mature, their leaves change colour from pink-tinted to green. Eventually they begin bearing fruit. Cupuaçu fruits are wikt:oblong|oblong, brown, and fuzzy, 20&nbsp;cm (8&nbsp;in) long, 1–2&nbsp;kg (2–4&nbsp;lb) in weight, and covered with a thick (4–7&nbsp;mm), hard exocarp.

The white Pulp (fruit)|pulp of the cupuaçu is uniquely fragrance|fragrant. It contains theacrine (1,3,7,9-tetramethyluric acid) instead of the xanthines (caffeine, theobromine, and theophylline) found in cacao. Asahi Foods, a Japanese company, once tried to patent the production and use of Cupuaçu seed fat, but they failed.

The wood is also commonly used for timber.
