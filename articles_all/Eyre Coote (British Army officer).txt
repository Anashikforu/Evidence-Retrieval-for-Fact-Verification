
'''Eyre Coote''' (20 May 1762 – 10 December 1823) was a United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland|British soldier and politician who was born in Ireland.

He was known as General (United Kingdom)|General Sir Eyre Coote until 1816; Order of the Bath|GCB 1815 - 1816.

Background
He was the second son of the Very Rev. Charles Coote (1713 - 12 February 1776), Doctor of Divinity|DD, Dean (Christianity)|Dean of Kilfenora.  After studying at Eton College|Eton and Trinity College Dublin, Coote bought a commission in the 34th Regiment of Foot - of which his uncle was colonel - in 1774.

He was soon sent to  North America to fight in the American Revolutionary War|Revolutionary War.  He fought in the Battle of Long Island|Brooklyn in August 1776, as well as at Battle of Brandywine|Brandywine, Battle of Germantown|Germantown and Battle of Monmouth|Monmouth Court House before finally being taken prisoner at the final Battle of Yorktown (1781)|Battle of Yorktown in 1781. Between 1790 and 1798, he represented Ballynakill (Parliament of Ireland constituency)|Ballynakill in the Irish House of Commons. Then he sat for Maryborough (Parliament of Ireland constituency)|Maryborough until 1800.

He served in England and Ireland when they were threatened by French invasion and against the French on the Continent. He went on to serve in Holland, Egypt and eventually became Governor-General of Jamaica (1806–1808) where he possibly sired an ancestor of Colin Powell.
Scandal and death
Sir Eyre was removed from the service on the 21 May 1816 because of the scandal he caused in the Christ's Hospital school for boys."William Cooper" (Pseudonym of James Glass Bertram) ''History of the Rod. Flagellation and the Flagellants'', published by John Camden Hotten, 1869; pp.522-527 On 25 November 1815 he entered the school and offered some boys money for an opportunity to flog them. After that he asked them to flog him and gave them money. He was caught by the school nurse, and charged with indecent conduct before the Lord Mayor of London.  He was acquitted after giving £1000 to the school:  after that there was a military inquiry in April 1816 which stripped him of his rank and honours.John Chandos, "Boys together: English public schools, 1800-1864", Hutchinson, 1984, , p.499 He was appointed Grand Cross in the Order of the Bath|GCB 1815 and stripped of it 1816.
