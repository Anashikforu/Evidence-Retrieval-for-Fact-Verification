

'''José de Sousa Saramago''', (pronounced|ʒuˈzɛ sɐɾɐˈmagu; born November 16, 1922; died June 18, 2010) was a Portugal|Portuguese writer, playwright and journalist.

Saramago was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1998. He lived on Lanzarote in the Canary Islands, Spain.

 Biography 
Saramago was born into a family of landless peasants in Azinhaga, Portugal, a small village in the province of Ribatejo some hundred kilometers north-east of Lisbon.

Saramago married Ilda Reis in 1944. Their only child, Violante, was born in 1947. Since 1988, Saramago has been married to the Spain|Spanish journalist Pilar del Río, who is the official translator of his books into Spanish.

José Saramago was in his mid-fifties before he had international success; his novel ''Baltasar and Blimunda'' brought him an international readership. This novel won the Portuguese PEN Club Award.

Saramago was a member of the Portuguese Communism|Communist Party from 1969, until his death 
 Themes 

Saramago uses for his works fantastic scenarios. In his 1986 novel, ''The Stone Raft'', the Iberian Peninsula breaks off from the rest of Europe and sails about the Atlantic Ocean. In his 1995 novel, ''Blindness'', an entire country is :wikt:strike|stricken with a mysterious plague called “white blindness”.
