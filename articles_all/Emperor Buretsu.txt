 Traditional history 
Buretsu was a son of Emperor Ninken.   Buretsu was childless; and he became been the last monarch of the first Imperial dynasty of Japan.Aston, William. (1998). ''Nihongi,'' Vol. 1, pp. 393-407.

 Events of Buretsu's life 
Very little is known about the events of Buretsu's life and reign.  Only limited information is wikt:available|available for study prior to the reign of the 29th monarch, Emperor Kimmei.Titsingh, pp. 34-36; Brown, pp. 261-262; Varley, pp. 123-124.

As emperor, Buretsu is described as wicked in the ''Nihonshoki,'' but this assessment is not mirrored in the text of the ''Kojiki.''

= After his death =
This emperor's official name after his death (his posthumous name) was wikt:regularize|regularized many centuries after the lifetime which was wikt:ascribe|ascribed to Buretsu.Aston (1998), pp. 146-147.

The ''kami'' of Emperor Buretsu was venerated at a Shinto shrine in Takaichi, Yamato province.Ponsonby-Fane, Richard. (1959). ''The Imperial House of Japan,'' p. 128.

According to the Imperial Household Agency, the emperor's final resting place is in an wikt:earthen|earthen tumulus (''kofun'').  Buretsu  is wikt:venerate|venerated at a memorial Shinto shrine (''misasagi'') which is associated with the burial mound.
