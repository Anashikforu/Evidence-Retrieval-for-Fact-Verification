

A '''furlong''' is a unit of measurement|unit for measuring distance. It is part of the imperial units|imperial and United States customary units|US customary systems. It is equal to . There are eight furlongs in a mile.

History
The unit was first used in the ninth century at the latest. The word "furlong" comes from Old English words ''furh'' ("furrow") and ''lang'' ("long"). Originally it was the length of the wiktionary:furrow|furrow in one acre of a ploughed field. An acre is an area that is one furlong long and one chain (unit)|chain wide. For this reason, the furlong was once also called an ''acre's length''. Around the year 1300, England standardized the furlong as 40 rod (unit)|rods or 660 feet.

The length of the furlong was standardized between South Africa, Australia, United Kingdom, Canada , New Zealand and the United States in the International Pound and Yard Agreement.

The furlong was abolished for official use in the UK in 1985. It is still occasionally seen on traffic sign|road signs in Myanmar.

Distances for horse racing in the UK, Ireland, and Canada are still given in miles and furlongs. The unit is not used otherwise. It is considered to be an ''archaic'' unit.

The proverb ''one furlong per fortnight'' means to express something in as obscure and little-used units as possible. (A fortnight is two weeks, or fourteen days). The proverb itself means speed, and is the same as 0.000166309524 m/s or 0.17 mm/s.
