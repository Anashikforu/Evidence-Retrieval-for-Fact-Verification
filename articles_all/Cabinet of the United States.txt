

The '''Cabinet of the United States''' (usually simplified as "the Cabinet") is composed of the most senior appointed officers of the executive branch of the federal government of the United States, and its existence dates back to the first United States of America|American President of the United States|President (George Washington), who appointed a Cabinet of four people (United States Secretary of State|Secretary of State, Thomas Jefferson; Secretary of the Treasury, Alexander Hamilton; United States Secretary of War|Secretary of War, Henry Knox; and United States Attorney General|Attorney General, Edmund Randolph) to advise and assist him in his duties.

Cabinet officers are nominated by the President and then presented to the United States Senate for Advice and consent|confirmation or rejection by a simple majority. If approved, they are Oath of office|sworn in and begin their duties. Aside from Attorney General, and previously, the United States Postmaster General|Postmaster General, they all receive the title ''Secretary''.
