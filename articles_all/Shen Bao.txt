

'''''Shen Bao''''' (Chinese language|Chinese: 申報; Pinyin: Shēn Bào) was a newspaper published in Shanghai, China. It was published  between April 30, 1872 and May 27, 1949, making it the longest running for any newspaper in China. The name has also been transliteration|transliterated as '''''Shun Pao''''' or '''''Shen-pao'''''. In English language|English, the newspaper was usually called '''''Shanghai News'''''. The name is short for ''Shenjiang Xinbao''. Shenjiang is a short form of Chunsheng jiang, the old name for the Huangpu River.Endymion Wilkinson, ''Chinese History: A Manual, Revised and Enlarged'' (Harvard University Asia Center, 2000: ), p. 967.
Establishment
The paper was started by Ernest Major (1841-1908),Dates from Roberta Wue, "The Profits of Philanthropy: Relief Aid, Shenbao, and the Art World in Later Nineteenth-Century Shanghai," ''Late Imperial China'' 25 (June 2004), pp. 187-211. in 1872. Major was a British businessman. ''Shen Bao'' was one of the first modern Chinese newspapers. Major returned to England in 1899, and the newspaper was reorganised to be Major Company Limited. According to Zhou,Yongming Zhou, ''Historicizing Online Politics: Telegraphy, the Internet, and Political Participation in China'' (Stanford University Press, 2006: ), p. 45. Major was different from other foreign newspapers in two areas: From the start, he made it clear, that the newspaper would be for Chinese readers, and not for foreigners. He therefore picked those news and issues that would be interesting to Chinese. Secondly, he made Chinese compradors responsible for running the business and he had Chinese editors select the news and write the editorials. He was very successful: The Chinese compradors used their ties with the community,  raised the Magazine circulation|circulation, and obtained advertisements. This made the paper cheaper than those of the competition. The Chinese editors knew what their readers wanted to read about.
Roles
''Shen Bao'' played an important role and helped form public opinion at the end of the 19th century. The paper ran a campaign against hiring young women as waitresses in opium dens. According to the campaign, this made it difficult to distinguish between what could be tolerated, and what could not, because it implicitly said these women would also provide prostitution|sex services in the dens. Most of the dens with this practice were in the Shanghai French Concession|French Concession. The campaign said that one of the reasons this problem existed was that there were foreigners in Shanghai.Paraphrased from Thou:Historizing politics, p. 50 There was an uproar, and the practice was banned; in reality little was done to eradicate it, though.

The newspaper was innovation|innovative: It used new printing technologies, sent a military correspondent to cover the Sino-French War in Vietnam in 1884 and used Vernacular Chinese|vernacular (''baihua'')"Wilkinson, ''Chinese History'', p. 967. Quickly it got the reputation of being one of the best newspapers in China. It was bought by a Chinese in 1909.Wilkinson, ''Chinese History'', p. 968. In the early 20th century it had a circulation of 30.000 copies a day. 9.000 of them in Shanhai, and the rest was for other parts of China.Mary Ninde Gamewell, ''New Life Currents in China'' (Missionary Education Movement of the United States and Canada, 1919), pp. 162-163. "By the early 1920s its circulation was 50,000; by the end of the decade 100,000; and by the mid 1930s, 150,000."Wilkinson, ''Chinese History'', p. 995.

Recent history
Shen Bao was founded as a commercial newspaper. Politically, it was conservatism|conservative for the first three decades and supported the Qing Dynasty|Qing government. In 1905, it started to change its orientation: On New Year's Day it quoted Liang Qichao's constitutionalist slogans. In 1907, the paper was sold  to Xi Zipei (1867-1929),Dates from Ellen Widmer, "of the paper. Xi Zipei was influenced by Zhang Jian (politician)|Zhang Jian. In 1934, the newspaper made the government angry, because it had a strong attitude against the Japanese.
